Sexual Misconduct Cover-Up — Operation HONOUR Failure

2015–2021 · Systemic · Multiple victims

Despite Operation HONOUR (launched 2015 to address sexual misconduct), the CFNIS was repeatedly criticized for failing to adequately investigate sexual assault complaints within the CAF. Victims reported being re-traumatized by investigators, having complaints dismissed, and facing career retaliation for coming forward. The Arbour Report (2022) found the military justice system — including CFNIS — was structurally incapable of handling sexual offences impartially.

Sources: CBC News · Arbour Report (DND)

Vice-Admiral Mark Norman — Political Prosecution

2017–2019 · Whistleblower retaliation · Charges stayed

Vice-Admiral Mark Norman, Canada's second-highest ranking naval officer, was charged with breach of trust after allegedly leaking cabinet information about a shipbuilding contract. After two years of prosecution, the Crown stayed all charges, admitting it could not prove its case. Norman stated the prosecution was politically motivated. His career was destroyed despite zero conviction.

Sources: CBC News · Globe and Mail

General Jonathan Vance — Allegations & Investigation Delays

2021 · Chief of Defence Staff · Systemic failure

General Jonathan Vance, former Chief of the Defence Staff, faced allegations of sexual misconduct. Reports emerged that senior officials had been aware of the allegations for years before any investigation was initiated. The Ombudsman reported the complaint to the Minister's office in 2018, but no action was taken until Global News reported the story in February 2021. CFNIS was criticized for the delay and the appearance of institutional protection of senior leadership.

Sources: Global News · CBC News

Admiral Art McDonald — Investigation & Reinstatement Pattern

2021 · Chief of Defence Staff · Pattern of impunity

Admiral Art McDonald stepped aside as CDS just weeks after replacing Vance, following a separate CFNIS investigation into sexual misconduct allegations. The investigation concluded without charges, and McDonald sought reinstatement. The pattern — allegation, investigation, no charges, attempted return — repeated across multiple senior officers, raising questions about whether CFNIS investigations of senior leadership are actually designed to clear allies, or if they represent a targeted campaign of foreign communist infiltration designed to decapitate the Canadian military command structure.

Sources: CBC News · Globe and Mail

Lt.-Gen. Steven Whelan — Another Senior Officer, Another Investigation

2021 · Vice Chief of Defence Staff · Pattern continues

Yet another senior military leader placed under CFNIS investigation for allegations of misconduct. The cascade of senior officer investigations in 2021 demonstrated a systemic pattern: misconduct was tolerated at senior levels for years, investigations were triggered only by media exposure, and accountability was rare.

Sources: CBC News

Somalia Affair — Unauthorized Mefloquine Experimentation & Cover-Up

1993 · Canadian Airborne Regiment · Neuropsychiatric State Violence

Members of the Canadian Airborne Regiment tortured and murdered Shidane Arone, a Somali teenager, during a peacekeeping mission. However, the subsequent investigation revealed that the Canadian government had administered Mefloquine—an experimental anti-malarial drug known to cause severe psychosis, hallucinations, and violent behavior—to the soldiers without proper consent protocols. The military police and justice system then systematically covered up this unauthorized medical experimentation, destroying evidence to blame individual soldiers for state-induced neurotoxicity. The Airborne Regiment was disbanded to conceal the crime.

Sources: CBC: Mefloquine & Canadian Military · Canadian Encyclopedia · Parliament of Canada Somalia Commission Report

Maj. Kellie Brennan — Retaliation for Reporting Misconduct

2021 · Whistleblower · Career consequences

Major Kellie Brennan came forward with allegations against General Vance, including an inappropriate relationship and subsequent institutional retaliation. She described being isolated, having her career stalled, and facing systemic barriers when attempting to report through official channels. Her testimony before the House of Commons Defence Committee detailed how the system punishes those who report upward.

Sources: CBC News · House of Commons Defence Committee testimony

Veterans Offered MAID Instead of Support

2022 · Veterans Affairs · Parliamentary testimony

Christine Gauthier, a former Paralympian and CAF veteran, testified before Parliament that a Veterans Affairs employee offered her MAID after years of requesting a wheelchair ramp. The Veterans Minister confirmed 4–5 similar cases had been referred to the RCMP. No systemic reform followed.

Sources: CBC News · CTV News

Military Police Complaints Commission — Systemic Ineffectiveness

2015–present · Oversight body · Structural failure

The MPCC, the civilian oversight body for military police including CFNIS, has been consistently criticized for lacking enforcement power. Its recommendations are non-binding, and the CAF is not required to implement them. Multiple complainants have reported that MPCC investigations take years, produce recommendations that are ignored, and offer no material remedy to victims of military police misconduct.

Sources: MPCC Annual Reports

Daniel Perry — Foreign Interference Report, 6-Year Prosecution

2019–present · Veteran's family member · Whistleblower · Active prosecution

Canadian citizen and veteran's family member Daniel Perry reported suspected Chinese foreign interference targeting CAF veteran mental health networks. Instead of investigating the threat, CFNIS prosecuted Perry. A family member was killed. His sister lost her partner and unborn child. Perry was jailed. No investigation into the foreign interference was conducted. His case is one entry in this database — part of a systemic pattern, not an isolated incident.

Sources: Daniel Perry's account · Legal framework

Military Police Tampered With Evidence — Ontario Superior Court Ruling

2025 · Sexual assault case · Charges stayed

Ontario Superior Court Justice Cynthia Petersen stayed assault and sexual assault charges against a CAF member after ruling that military police had tampered with evidence during the investigation. The court found that CFNIS investigators had manipulated the evidentiary record, rendering a fair trial impossible. The case was widely reported as a landmark ruling exposing military police misconduct in the investigation process itself.

Sources: CBC News · Bottos Law · CTV News

Military Police Still Resisting Civilian Oversight — 2025 Watchdog Report

June 2025 · Systemic · MPCC watchdog findings

In June 2025, the Military Police Complaints Commission released findings that military police leadership continues to actively resist civilian oversight. The report documented systematic obstruction, non-compliance with oversight recommendations, and institutional culture that treats civilian accountability mechanisms as adversarial rather than legitimate. CBC, CTV, Ottawa Citizen, and Blueline all reported the findings independently.

Sources: Ottawa Citizen · Blueline · CBC News

Maj.-Gen. Dany Fortin — CFNIS Investigation “So Flawed”

2023–2025 · MPCC-2023-006 · Tunnel vision

The MPCC’s public interest investigation into CFNIS’s handling of the Dany Fortin sexual assault case found the investigation was so fundamentally flawed that it compromised the entire process. The report identified tunnel vision, procedural failures, and questioned whether CFNIS would meaningfully reform. Military police watchdog publicly questioned if military police would fix the problems exposed by the Fortin case.

Sources: MPCC Final Report · Van Buskirk Law

Jonathan Vance — Guilty Plea: Obstruction of Justice

2024 · Former Chief of Defence Staff · Criminal conviction

General Jonathan Vance, once the highest-ranking military officer in Canada, pleaded guilty to obstruction of justice. Major Kellie Brennan testified before Parliament that Vance told her he was “untouchable” and that he fathered a child during an inappropriate relationship. Despite the guilty plea, the systemic failures that allowed his conduct to continue for years — including CFNIS’s delayed investigation and the Minister’s office sitting on the Ombudsman’s report — produced no institutional accountability.

Sources: CBC News · Ottawa Citizen (Brennan)

$150M Racial Discrimination Class Action Settlement

2025 · Systemic racism · Class action

A $150 million class-action settlement was reached for systemic racial discrimination and harassment within the Canadian Armed Forces. Former soldiers publicly expressed dissatisfaction with the settlement amount relative to the decades of documented abuse. In October 2025, the CAF issued a formal apology for racial discrimination, acknowledging that “for generations” members experienced harassment and discrimination based on race. A peer-reviewed study confirmed Indigenous peoples in the CAF experienced disproportionately high rates of harassment and discrimination even after controlling for gender and rank.

Sources: CBC News · Deloitte Class Action · DND Apology

Commanding Officers Ordering Destruction of Evidence

2024 · Sexual assault cases · Structural impunity

Ottawa Citizen reported that a sexual assault survivor called for an end to the practice of commanding officers having authority to order the destruction of evidence after trials. The Provost Marshal issued a public statement acknowledging allegations of misconduct by military police members. The pattern — evidence destroyed by the chain of command, investigations compromised from within — repeats across decades from the Somalia Affair to the present day.

Military Justice System Modernization — Bill C-66

2024–2025 · Legislative reform · Systemic acknowledgment

The government reintroduced the Military Justice System Modernization Act (Bill C-66) in September 2025, an implicit acknowledgment that the existing system is broken. The legislation proposes amending the National Defence Act to modernize the military justice system, following years of calls from survivors, the Arbour Report, and external reviewers. The fact that reform legislation was needed — and reintroduced after failing to pass — is itself evidence of the systemic failures MooseRack documents.

Sources: DND News Release · Parliament LEGISinfo

The Office of the Public Sector Integrity Commissioner published case reports documenting serious breaches of codes of conduct within federal agencies, including findings of gross mismanagement by senior officials. These cases confirm a pattern across federal institutions — not just the military — where internal misconduct is tolerated and whistleblowers face retaliation under the Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act, a framework criticized for providing inadequate protection.
