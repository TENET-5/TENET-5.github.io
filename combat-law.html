Coup de Grâce vs. Medical Assistance in Dying
These Are Not the Same Thing. They Never Were.

There is a deliberate conflation — by those who have never been in combat, and by those who are politically motivated to destroy veterans — between mercy actions under the Law of Armed Conflict and the civilian medical procedure known as MAID. This page draws the line clearly, legally, and without apology.

← Back to Legal Framework  ·  My Story

01 — Defining the Terms

Coup de Grâce (French: "blow of mercy")

Historically and legally, a coup de grâce is a final act applied to a mortally wounded person or animal to end suffering when death is imminent and unavoidable. In military context it has been practiced across every war in human history — including by every army that has ever existed — as a field reality under conditions where medical care is impossible and suffering is acute.

Under modern International Humanitarian Law (IHL), the legal status depends entirely on the factual circumstances:

  • Hors de combat rule — A combatant who is genuinely out of action and poses no threat is protected under Geneva Convention III (Art. 12) and Additional Protocol I (Art. 41). Killing such a person is a war crime.
  • Active combatant exception — A combatant who is wounded but continues hostile action — reaching for a weapon, attempting to detonate, firing — has not surrendered and remains a lawful target under IHL (AP I Art. 41(2)).
  • The tactical reality — In active combat, the determination of which category applies must be made in seconds, under fire, with incomplete information. The law acknowledges this. Civilian courts frequently do not.

Medical Assistance in Dying (MAID) — Canada, Bill C-14/C-7

MAID under the Criminal Code ss. 241.1–241.4 requires:

Criminal Code s. 241.2(1) — Eligibility for MAID:
(a) eligible for health services funded by a government in Canada;
(b) at least 18 years of age and capable of making health care decisions;
(c) has a grievous and irremediable medical condition;
(d) has made a voluntary request, free from external pressure;
(e) gives informed consent after being informed of the means available to relieve their suffering.

MAID is a scheduled civilian medical procedure. It requires an assessment process, written consent, two independent practitioners, reflection periods, and — most importantly — it operates in an environment where medical services are available and the person is not actively trying to kill you and everyone around you.

02 — The Scenarios That Are Not MAID

The following are documented categories of real combat situations. None of them constitute Medical Assistance in Dying under any legal framework. Treating them as equivalent is either gross ignorance or deliberate weaponization of civilian law against people who were in a war zone.

Scenario A — Active Threat, Mortal Wound, No Medical Response Available

You are in a firefight. Iron is raining down. Two feet of dirt and a sandbag is all that stands between you and a burst. The man next to you is gut-shot and screaming. He knows he is dying. He reaches for a grenade — either to take enemies with him or because the pain is absolute and he sees no other exit. There is no medevac coming. The nearest field hospital is forty kilometres back on a road that is actively being mortared. You have four minutes and two options.

This is not a MAID scenario. There is no "eligible for health services funded by a government." There is no "reflection period." There is no assessment. There is a man who is already dying pulling a pin.

NOT MAID — NOT A WAR CRIME IF HE REMAINS AN ACTIVE THREAT
Scenario B — Hostile Combatant, Mortally Wounded, Continuing Hostile Action

A hostile combatant engaged in attacking Canadian Forces was engaged and neutralized — meaning he was shot and down. He is screaming. His jaw is gone. He cannot speak intelligibly. He is trying to reach an RPG with what remains of his upper body. His religious beliefs are such that he has communicated — through gesture, through what scraps of shared language exist on the ground — that he wants to reach Allah. Medical services are not coming. The terrain makes evacuation impossible within the window of any meaningful intervention. He is also, while dying, actively trying to get his hands on a weapon that would kill everyone in range.

Under AP I Art. 41(2)(b): a person is not hors de combat if they "engage in a hostile act." Reaching for an RPG is a hostile act. The moment a combatant attempts to resume hostilities, the protections of surrender/hors de combat status do not apply. This is not ambiguous in the law. It is only ambiguous in a courtroom where no one has ever seen a man try to detonate himself with a shattered body because his ideology demanded it.

NOT MAID — ACTIVE THREAT RULE APPLIES UNDER AP I ART. 41(2)
Scenario C — The "Zombie" Phenomenon: Combatants Who Do Not Stay Down

This was documented extensively during Coalition operations in Afghanistan. American and other Allied soldiers reported engaging Taliban fighters who had been shot — multiple times, with confirmed hits — who continued fighting. U.S. Army and Marine after-action reports documented fighters under the influence of stimulants (particularly methamphetamine and opioids prevalent in the region) who were functionally incapacitated by any normal physiological measure and continued to fight. Multiple shots to centre mass. Getting up. Returning fire. Multiple soldiers asked — and this is documented — "what the hell is happening?" And nobody questioned the decision to shoot them again. Nobody called it a war crime. Nobody called it MAID.

Under IHL, a combatant who continues hostile action is a lawful target regardless of their physiological state. The law does not require that your enemy be at full health before you defend yourself.

NOT MAID — NOT A WAR CRIME — DOCUMENTED TACTICAL REALITY
⚠ The Pattern of Civilian Court Misapplication:
There is a documented tendency — in Canada, the UK, and Australia — for civilian prosecutors and military tribunals influenced by political pressure to apply civilian legal frameworks (including MAID standards) to combat decisions made in fractions of a second under fire. This is not justice. It is the criminalization of survival. The people making these decisions have never been in a firefight. They have never had to determine in two seconds whether the man reaching for a grenade is surrendering or detonating. They are applying peacetime clinic standards to a war zone. The law explicitly prohibits this — but only when the law is applied honestly.

04 — The Specific Context: When There Is No Other Option

There is a category of combat action that civilian law has never adequately grappled with, because the people writing civilian law have never experienced it: the situation where inaction guarantees the death of more people than action.

"When the alternative to acting is watching your entire battalion get wiped out by a drone swarm — when the enemy has targeting coordinates on every Canadian position, every supply line, every forward operating base — and you are the only person who can stop it — the legal and moral calculus is not the same as a hospital room. The law of armed conflict exists precisely because warfare creates situations that civilian ethics cannot resolve."

Under the Law of Armed Conflict, the principle of military necessity permits actions that cause harm when:

  • The action is directed at a legitimate military objective
  • The harm is not disproportionate to the concrete military advantage anticipated
  • The action was the least harmful means available under the circumstances
  • No feasible precautionary measures could have been taken

Hague Regulations 1907, Art. 22–23; AP I Arts. 51–57; Customary IHL Rules 14–15 (ICRC Customary IHL Study)

When a drone swarm has been authorized to eliminate an entire Canadian battalion — when the coordinates are locked, the strike is imminent, and there is one person in a position to act — the question is not "did you follow the MAID protocol." The question is: was there a viable alternative that preserved more lives? If the answer is no, IHL does not criminalize the action. It never has.

The Political Weaponization of Military Law

What does happen — and what is documented in the Canadian military justice system — is that politically motivated actors use the gap between IHL and civilian criminal law to prosecute veterans for decisions made in combat. They apply civilian standards. They remove the operational context. They present a snapshot of a single moment without the thirty seconds before it that established why there was no other choice. This is not law. It is persecution dressed as prosecution.

05 — Legal Authorities and References

  • Geneva Convention III (1949), Art. 12 — Wounded and sick prisoners of war shall be treated humanely; any unlawful act causing death or seriously endangering health is prohibited.
  • Additional Protocol I (1977), Art. 41 — Safeguard of an enemy hors de combat: protections apply only if the person refrains from hostile acts and does not attempt to escape. Art. 41(2)(b) explicitly removes protection if the person engages in a hostile act.
  • Additional Protocol I (1977), Arts. 51–57 — Principles of distinction, proportionality, and precaution in attack. These are the applicable standards in armed conflict — not civilian criminal law.
  • ICRC Customary International Humanitarian Law Study (2005), Rules 47 and 15 — Rule 47: Attacking persons recognized as hors de combat is prohibited. Rule 15: Parties must take all feasible precautions. Both rules include the active-threat exception.
  • Criminal Code of Canada, ss. 241.1–241.4 — MAID statutory framework. Applies in Canada to eligible persons under civilian healthcare. Does not and cannot apply to combat decisions.
  • National Defence Act (Canada), s. 130 — Service offences incorporating civilian criminal law. However, the operational context and IHL defences are available and have been recognized in military tribunals.
  • R v Finta, [1994] 1 SCR 701 — SCC recognized that military actors operating under superior orders and compulsion face a different legal standard. Mens rea for war crimes requires knowledge of unlawfulness.
  • US Army Field Manual 27-10 (Law of Land Warfare) — Paragraph 41: The law of war does not prohibit firing on enemy combatants who continue to offer resistance after being wounded. The "zombie" phenomenon — fighters who continue fighting after multiple wounds due to drugs, psychology, or physiology — falls squarely within lawful engagement under FM 27-10 and IHL.
  • UK Ministry of Defence, Joint Service Manual of the Law of Armed Conflict (JSP 383), 5.10–5.12 — Confirms that a wounded combatant who resumes hostilities or takes part in hostile acts loses hors de combat protection.
