🪶 Residential Schools — From an Indigenous Veteran Who Actually Read the Data

I'm Indigenous. I served this country. And I'm going to say what most people are too scared to say.

The residential school system was real. It was systemic. Children suffered and died in those institutions. Nobody with a functioning conscience disputes that. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission heard testimony from nearly 7,000 survivors and issued 94 Calls to Action. That process mattered.

But here's what also matters — the political manipulation of that tragedy.

The GPR Scans — And Who Ran Them

After the announcement of 215 potential unmarked graves at Kamloops in 2021, SNC-Lavalin — the same company convicted of fraud, the same company caught spending money on escorts and prostitutes — sent letters to over 50 First Nations offering to run ground-penetrating radar scans.

At the Thunderchild Indian Residential School in Delmas, Saskatchewan, SNC-Lavalin ran the initial GPR scan. Despite 44 documented child deaths at that school, the scan found no graves. As of 2024, no bodies have been exhumed at any of the sites announced across the country.

Ground-penetrating radar detects soil anomalies — not bodies. It cannot confirm a burial. It can only flag disturbances that might be graves. Every expert who works with GPR says this. Yet the initial announcements were reported globally as confirmed mass graves.

The Mortality Rates Nobody Talks About

Here's what I found when I looked at the actual historical data:

In 1900, the infant mortality rate across all of Canada was 187 per 1,000 live births. In Montreal, it was 250 per 1,000. In Aboriginal communities like Fisher River, Manitoba, it was 249 per 1,000 between 1910 and 1939. One in three to one in four children died before age five — everywhere.

Orphanages, workhouses, and industrial schools across the country had comparable or worse death rates. Children died in institutions because institutional care in the 19th and early 20th century was catastrophic for all children, regardless of race. Disease, malnutrition, overcrowding, and medical ignorance killed children in every institution that existed.

That doesn't excuse what happened. It means the residential school system was part of a systemic institutional failure — not a uniquely racial extermination program. As an Indigenous person, I can say that: the system was wrong, the forced separation was wrong, the cultural destruction was real. But the framing that this was racial genocide distinct from what happened to every other institutionalized child in that era doesn't match the data.

The Voices That Were Silenced

Senator Lynn Beyak was removed from the Senate Aboriginal Peoples Committee in 2017 for saying there was good alongside the bad in the schools. Whether you agree with her or not, she was silenced for adding nuance to the conversation. That's not reconciliation — that's narrative control.

Teachers who worked in the later stages of the system — many of whom genuinely tried to help — were never given a platform. Their testimony was excluded from the dominant narrative. Conservative voices were systematically shut out of the conversation, not because they were wrong, but because they complicated the political utility of the story.

The residential school system followed the exact pattern I described above. The program existed for decades. Witnesses who spoke up were silenced. The truth eventually emerged. A commission was formed. Canada apologized — generations too late for the children who died.

But now that same tragedy is being weaponized. Churches burned. Statues torn down. A convicted fraud company running the forensic scans. And anyone who asks questions gets called a denier.

That's not truth. That's not reconciliation. That's political manipulation of dead children. And the people doing it are sick in the head.
