02 — WHO IS DYINGThe Vulnerable, Not the Terminally Ill

In 2024, 732 people (4.4%) who received MAID were on Track 2 — meaning their death was not reasonably foreseeable. These are not dying patients choosing dignity. These are people whose suffering is rooted in poverty, isolation, and systemic failure.[6]

Among Track 2 recipients in 2024:[6]

  • 47% reported suffering from loneliness and isolation
  • 49% reported feeling like a burden on family, friends, or caregivers
  • Track 2 recipients are disproportionately from socially disadvantaged neighbourhoods[7]
Suffering leading to MAID requests is often rooted not in a person's medical condition, but in the systemic barriers they face: poverty, inaccessible housing, lack of care, and social exclusion.[7]

03 — VETERANSThey Offered Death Instead of a Wheelchair Ramp

In December 2022, former Paralympian and Canadian Armed Forces veteran Christine Gauthier testified before Parliament that a Veterans Affairs Canada (VAC) employee offered her MAID after she spent years requesting a wheelchair ramp for her home.[8][9]

Veterans Minister Lawrence MacAulay confirmed that at least four to five cases of veterans being offered MAID by a VAC employee had been referred to the RCMP.[9]

Prime Minister Trudeau called the employee's conduct "absolutely unacceptable" — yet no systemic reform followed.[9]

A veteran who served her country as a Paralympian was told: if you are desperate enough, we can help you die. She had asked for a wheelchair ramp.

04 — INTERNATIONAL LAWWhen a State Kills Its Own Vulnerable Citizens

Under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (to which Canada is a signatory), crimes against humanity include acts committed as part of a widespread or systematic attack directed against a civilian population, with knowledge of the attack. Relevant acts include:

  • Article 7(1)(a) — Murder
  • Article 7(1)(k) — Other inhumane acts causing great suffering or serious injury to body or mental/physical health
  • Article 7(2)(a) — "Attack directed against a civilian population" means a course of conduct involving the multiple commission of acts pursuant to or in furtherance of a State policy

The question is not whether MAID is legal under Canadian domestic law. The question is whether a State program that has killed 76,475 of its own citizens — disproportionately the poor, the isolated, the disabled, and the elderly — constitutes a systematic attack on a civilian population under international law.

The UN Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities has repeatedly raised concerns about Canada's MAID regime and its impact on disabled persons who lack adequate social support.[10]
